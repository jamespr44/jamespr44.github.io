About

Who I Am

I came to engineering as a kid who couldn't leave things alone. Taking things apart, asking why systems were designed the way they were. That curiosity led me to Mechanical Engineering at UTS, and it still drives me every day.

My goal is clear: work on projects with tangible impact. Real, physical infrastructure that improves how cities function.

These experiences showed me that engineering is technically demanding, commercially driven, and deeply dependent on clear communication across disciplines. That's the work I want to keep doing.

Reflection

What I Learned at Fredon

Two years of hands-on experience in building services contracting — the expectations, surprises, and lessons that will shape my career.

Most Important Lessons

The two most important lessons were stakeholder communication and subcontractor management. On communication, I quickly learned that clearly conveying technical information to people with varying levels of engineering knowledge was both the hardest and most critical skill. Misunderstandings cost time and money. On subcontractor management, keeping multiple parties accountable requires firmness and relationship-building in equal measure. These skills cannot be taught in a classroom.

Career Direction

My internship has firmly pointed me toward a career in project management within building services or construction. Seeing how effective project management directly determines whether a project is delivered on time and within budget — and experiencing the satisfaction of driving that outcome myself — confirmed this is the work I want to pursue. The breadth of technical and commercial exposure I have gained places me in a strong position relative to most graduating students.

Expectations Before Starting

Before beginning at Fredon, I had limited insight into what working in building services would look like. I assumed it would differ significantly from university — given that commercial engineering is largely financially driven. I anticipated needing to develop financial literacy quickly and expected significant on-site time. Much of the day-to-day reality was unknown.

The Reality

The experience largely aligned with my expectations. The engineering work was more high-level than anticipated — rather than hands-on calculations, the focus was on system implementation, design compliance, and working efficiently within established frameworks. What surprised me was the scale of commercial and procurement exposure: contract negotiations, budget management, and subcontractor coordination.

Value to an Employer

My value proposition is the combination of technical engineering knowledge and hands-on project management experience at a level uncommon for a graduating student. Having independently run a fit-out project, contributed to major infrastructure at Sydney Metro Gadigal Station, helped design and manage the Sydney Biomedical Accelerator and the Waldorf Astoria mechanical package, and managed subcontractors and procurement across multiple live projects, I can demonstrate the ability to add real value from day one. My track record of operating with minimal supervision and receiving commendation for my communication are concrete examples I can speak to in any professional setting.
